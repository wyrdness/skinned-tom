## Overview

Skinned Tom is an urban legend and cautionary tale from the American South, with versions recorded in Tennessee, Alabama, Mississippi, and parts of Georgia. The core narrative involves a man — typically described as a charming womanizer or adulterer — who is discovered in an affair by a jealous husband or family member and is violently killed, typically by being flayed or skinned alive. His spirit returns as a humanoid figure entirely devoid of skin, exposing raw red musculature and sinew, typically carrying a long knife (either the blade used to flay him or a hunting knife taken from his killer). The creature haunts parked cars, motel parking lots, and remote roads at night, particularly locations associated with romantic trysts. The legend functions as an explicit moral warning: do not cheat on your partner, do not engage in secret affairs, and do not linger in isolated romantic spots after dark. Like many Southern Gothic cautionary legends, Skinned Tom combines lurid horror imagery with a strict moral framework, using the supernatural to enforce social norms around fidelity and discretion. The legend has limited documentation in academic folklore archives but has been transmitted orally through generations of Southern communities and appears in contemporary internet folklore discussions.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Vengeful spirit of a murder victim** _(Oral tradition)_

Within the legend's own framework, Skinned Tom is the literal returning spirit of a man murdered through flaying, who haunts the landscape of his transgression and targets those engaged in similar moral failures.

### Skeptical

**Cautionary social control mechanism** _(Folklore scholars, Urban legend researchers)_

Skinned Tom is a deliberately horrifying cautionary tale created and transmitted to deter infidelity and the use of isolated locations for secret romantic encounters. The grotesque imagery of flayed skin functions to make the moral warning as viscerally memorable as possible.
